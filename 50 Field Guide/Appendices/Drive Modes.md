# Drive Modes

## Purpose

Explain Canon R5 drive modes and when to use single, continuous, silent, and self-timer options.

## What it Does

Drive mode controls how many frames the camera captures per shutter press and whether delays or silent capture modes are used.

## How it Works

Single Shot captures one frame. Low Continuous, High Continuous, and High+ Continuous capture bursts at increasing rates. Silent modes depend on electronic shutter behavior. Self Timer delays capture.

## Advantages

- Single Shot encourages timing and conserves storage.
- Low Continuous supports controlled sequences.
- High and High+ Continuous capture peak action.
- Silent modes reduce disturbance.
- Self Timer reduces camera shake.

## Disadvantages

- High burst rates create many files.
- Silent/electronic drive can have rolling shutter limits.
- Continuous drive can hide timing mistakes.
- Self Timer is too slow for action.

## Recommended Uses

Use Single Shot for static scenes. Use Low Continuous for portraits or mild movement. Use High/High+ for action, birds, wildlife, and sports. Use Self Timer for tripod work.

## When Not to Use

Avoid High+ when storage, buffer, or editing load matters. Avoid silent electronic drive with fast motion or flickering light.

## Decision Guide

Static: Single Shot. Gentle movement: Low Continuous. Action: High or High+. Tripod: Self Timer or remote. Need silence: Silent mode if shutter limitations are acceptable.

## Recommended Settings by Profile

- Birds in Flight: High Speed Continuous+.
- Camera Defaults: Single Shooting.
- Sports: High Continuous or High+.
- Wildlife: High Speed Continuous for behavior/action.

## Canon-Specific Notes

On the Canon R5, drive mode interacts with shutter mode, buffer, card speed, and subject movement.

## Tips

Use short bursts. Match drive mode to the moment, not the subject category alone.

## Common Mistakes

- Leaving High+ on for static scenes.
- Forgetting silent mode limitations.
- Filling the buffer before peak action.
