Flash Photography

Purpose

Explain Canon R5 flash concepts and practical choices for E-TTL, manual flash, sync modes, and triggers.

What it Does

Flash adds controlled light to a scene, either as primary light, fill, or a timed creative accent.

How it Works

E-TTL meters a preflash automatically. Manual flash uses a fixed power. Sync modes determine when the flash fires relative to the shutter.

Advantages

  • E-TTL adapts quickly.
  • Manual flash is consistent.
  • Flash Exposure Compensation fine-tunes automatic flash.
  • High Speed Sync allows wider apertures in bright light.
  • Rear Curtain Sync places motion trails behind subjects.

Disadvantages

  • HSS reduces power.
  • E-TTL can vary between frames.
  • Manual flash requires distance/power adjustment.
  • Multiple flash and wireless flash add setup complexity.

Recommended Uses

Use fill flash for people and travel. Use bounce flash indoors. Use manual flash for repeatable portraits, macro, and products. Use wireless flash for multi-light control.

When Not to Use

Avoid flash when it is prohibited, distracting, too weak for the distance, or incompatible with the selected shutter mode.

Decision Guide

Fast event: E-TTL. Controlled setup: Manual. Bright daylight wide aperture: HSS. Motion trail: Rear Curtain Sync. Remote timing: external trigger or Pluto trigger.

Recommended Settings by Profile

  • People: E-TTL fill or bounce flash.
  • Macro: manual flash with diffuser.
  • Travel: small fill flash when allowed.
  • Fireworks: generally no flash for distant bursts.

Canon-Specific Notes

Canon Speedlite systems support E-TTL, wireless control, Flash Exposure Compensation, and HSS. Flash generally requires Mechanical shutter on the R5.

Tips

Bounce when possible. Start with negative Flash Exposure Compensation for subtle fill. Check recycle time.

Common Mistakes

  • Expecting flash to light distant subjects.
  • Forgetting shutter sync limits.
  • Using HSS unnecessarily.
  • Leaving Flash Exposure Compensation from a prior scene.
