Metering Modes

Purpose

Explain Evaluative, Partial, Spot, and Center-weighted metering and when each mode helps.

What it Does

Metering determines how the camera reads scene brightness to recommend or set exposure.

How it Works

Evaluative analyzes the whole frame. Partial reads a central area. Spot reads a small central area. Center-weighted averages the frame with priority near the center.

Advantages

  • Evaluative: best general-purpose choice.
  • Partial: useful when the subject is central and backlit.
  • Spot: precise measurement of a small tone.
  • Center-weighted: predictable for classic centered compositions.

Disadvantages

  • Evaluative can be influenced by subject recognition and scene contrast.
  • Partial and Spot require careful placement.
  • Center-weighted can under- or over-expose off-center subjects.

Recommended Uses

Use Evaluative by default. Use Partial or Spot for difficult contrast. Use Center-weighted when you want a stable, traditional meter pattern.

When Not to Use

Avoid Spot unless you know exactly which tone you are metering. Avoid Center-weighted for off-center subjects unless exposure lock is used.

Decision Guide

General scene: Evaluative. Backlit central subject: Partial. Small known tone: Spot. Simple centered scene: Center-weighted.

Recommended Settings by Profile

  • Camera Defaults: Evaluative.
  • Fireworks: Manual exposure; metering is secondary.
  • Landscape: Evaluative with exposure compensation as needed.
  • People: Evaluative or Partial for backlight.
  • Wildlife: Evaluative, with compensation for bright/dark subjects.

Canon-Specific Notes

Canon Evaluative metering is highly capable and usually the safest starting point on the R5.

Tips

Use exposure compensation when the scene is mostly bright or mostly dark.

Common Mistakes

  • Using Spot accidentally.
  • Forgetting exposure compensation for snow, dark stages, or backlight.
  • Treating the meter as a creative decision instead of a starting point.
